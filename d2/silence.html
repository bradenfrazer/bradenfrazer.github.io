Silence

Looking at silence gives us a new perspective to question how different our lives would be without sound at all, and show us things that we hear every day without realizing it. It allows us to put ourselves in the shoes of another person, such as a deaf person, and dramatically alter our perception of sound for the rest of our lives.


A World Without Sound
The above linked blog is a post on what life is like as a deaf person. Bill gives some different insights on the both disadvantages and advantages of being deaf, and how he experiences his life every day filled with complete silence. Reading the blog gives some perspective to the small things we hear every day that we take for granted, and how life could be different if we didn't hear those things.



Silence: Lectures and Writings is a book by John Cage, an experimental composer. Silence is collection of essays and lectures Cage wrote during the period from 1939 to 1961, and contains new profound ideas about how one sees, thinks about, and hears the world. His revolution is best represented by his composition 4'33", a piano piece where the pianst does not make a sound at the instrument.

The concept of expressionism, as discussed in class, is that people can and will find many ways to express themselves. In the context of sound, audio mixing, playing instruments, and music in general are great examples of expressionism. However, silence remains an inverse way of expression too. For example, a prof that walks into a room and stares at the class without saying a word could be expressing anger or upset while being completely silent doing so.
